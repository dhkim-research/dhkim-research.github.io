Today, we spend 90% of our lives indoors, yet our buildings remain static—optimized for a theoretical 'average' user.

However, rapid developments in environmental sensing now allow for high-accuracy, spatially and temporally resolved physical comfort measurements. My research combines these technologies with machine learning to engineer adaptive environments that move beyond current standards to prioritize individual needs—toward smarter, healthier buildings.

About me

Scientist & Engineer | Building Physics & Illumination

I am a scientist and engineer trained at University College London (UCL), where my path evolved from architectural design to the physics of the built environment. My expertise is built on a PhD in Building Physics (2018) and an MSc in Light and Lighting (2012), yet fundamentally grounded in the architectural mindset developed during my BSc (2011).

My research lies at the interface of technology and human perception, with a dedicated emphasis on how daylight shapes the occupant experience. Focused on fundamental questions of visual comfort and health, I explore the dynamic interplay between space, light, and time—aiming to bridge architectural design with building physics.

I approach these challenges with a reverse-engineering mindset, using engineering as the language to translate complex human needs into quantifiable, responsive systems. My technical focus enhances architectural design intuition with physics and technologies—leveraging sensor technologies, machine learning, and predictive modeling to create responsive environments.

Currently, I am a Postdoctoral Researcher at ETH Zürich advancing the Solskin project—an adaptive façade system composed of orientable photovoltaic modules. I develop control frameworks that synthesize machine learning with real-time physical measurements, dynamically adjusting the shading to balance on-site energy generation with human comfort. Previously, I held postdoctoral positions at EPFL’s LIPID Lab (2021–2024) and TU Delft’s Indoor Environment Group (2018–2020).
